Section 1 — Overview: The Six Active Class I Railroads

As of 2026, six Class I freight railroads operate approximately 94,000 route miles across North America. The U.S. rail network is functionally divided into Western and Eastern halves, with interchange gateways concentrated along the Mississippi River corridor. Western roads — BNSF and UP — dominate the region west of Chicago and the Mississippi; Eastern roads — CSX and NS — cover the densely populated and industrialized Eastern U.S. The two international carriers, CN and CPKC, bridge Canada, the U.S., and (in CPKC's case) Mexico on north-south corridors.

Section 2 — UP Union Pacific Railroad

The largest U.S. railroad by revenue, UP Union Pacific operates west of Chicago and New Orleans across 23 states. The modern UP was built through a series of major acquisitions: Southern Pacific (1996), Missouri Pacific (1982), Missouri-Kansas-Texas (1988), and Chicago & North Western (1995). Headquarters: Omaha, Nebraska.

Houston Directional Lines

Houston is a major UP hub centered on Englewood Yard. Lines radiate in all cardinal directions, serving the Port of Houston and the Gulf Coast petrochemical and chemical corridor — one of the densest rail freight markets in the world.

  • West — Sunset Route (ex-SP): Houston → San Antonio → El Paso → Tucson → Los Angeles. Primary transcontinental link to Southern California ports and markets.
  • North — Ex-MoPac Line: Houston → Palestine → Fort Worth/Dallas → Kansas City or northwest. From Fort Worth/Dallas: north to KC, then Omaha, then Chicago (via ex-C&NW) or Pacific Northwest.
  • East: Houston → Beaumont → Lake Charles → New Orleans. UP and BNSF share parallel tracks on the Houston–New Orleans segment via directional running. Interchange with NS, CSX, and CN at New Orleans.
  • Northeast / North-Central: Houston → Longview → Little Rock → Memphis. Interchange with NS and CSX at Memphis. Houston → Shreveport → interchange with CPKC. Houston → Pine Bluff → St. Louis.

Note on UP-NS Merger: If the pending STB merger application (filed December 2025) is approved, UP would absorb NS's eastern network, creating the first single-line transcontinental railroad and eliminating Chicago/Mississippi River interchange delays on east-west traffic.

Section 3 — BNSF BNSF Railway

The largest U.S. railroad by track mileage and second largest by revenue. BNSF operates in 28 western states and is the dominant intermodal carrier, major grain hauler, and largest coal hauler in North America. Owned by Berkshire Hathaway since 2010. Formed from the 1995 merger of Burlington Northern and Atchison, Topeka & Santa Fe.

Named Transcontinental Corridors

Southern Transcon (Primary Intermodal Highway): LA/Long Beach → Cajon Pass → Barstow → Needles → Albuquerque → Amarillo → Wichita → Kansas City → Fort Madison → Chicago. Approximately 2,200 miles, nearly 100% double-tracked. Handles ~300 trains daily at peak. BNSF's premier intermodal franchise. J.B. Hunt is a key partner on this corridor.

Northern Transcon: Chicago → Twin Cities → Fargo → Minot → Havre → Glacier Park → Spokane → Seattle or Portland. Primary traffic: grain from the Northern Great Plains to Pacific Northwest export terminals, intermodal, and coal.

Central Corridor / Transcon: Multiple variants through Nebraska and Colorado. Includes BNSF trackage rights on ex-SP lines (Houston–New Orleans), granted as a condition of the UP-SP merger in 1996. BNSF operates directional running with UP on the Houston–New Orleans segment.

Section 4 — CSX CSX Transportation

CSX operates approximately 21,000 miles across the Eastern U.S. and is the dominant carrier serving the Southeast and Florida. The modern CSX descends from the 1980 merger of Chessie System (Baltimore & Ohio, Chesapeake & Ohio) and Seaboard System, plus the 1999 division of Conrail between CSX and NS. Headquarters: Jacksonville, Florida.

Section 5 — NS Norfolk Southern Railway

NS Norfolk Southern operates approximately 21,000 miles in the Eastern U.S. and is the dominant carrier in the central and southern Appalachian coal fields. The modern NS was formed by the 1982 merger of Norfolk & Western (N&W) and Southern Railway, plus the 1999 acquisition of roughly half of Conrail. Headquarters: Atlanta, Georgia.

Section 6 — CN Canadian National Railway

CN is primarily a north-south carrier in the United States, operating the ex-Illinois Central main line from Chicago straight south to New Orleans via Memphis and Jackson, MS. CN acquired the Illinois Central in 1998. It also has significant operations in Michigan, Minnesota, Wisconsin, and Alabama. U.S. route miles: approximately 6,400 of a total ~20,000 network.

Section 7 — CPKC Canadian Pacific Kansas City

CPKC was formed by the landmark 2023 merger of Canadian Pacific and Kansas City Southern, creating the only single-line railroad connecting Canada, the United States, and Mexico. The STB granted final approval in April 2023. CPKC is now the third-largest North American railroad by route miles (~20,000 miles across three countries).

CPKC Key Infrastructure Points

  • Laredo, TX — Patrick J. Ottensmeyer International Railway Bridge: Second span completed 2024, doubles cross-border rail capacity. Laredo is the busiest land border crossing for rail in North America.
  • Myrtlewood, AL (new 2024): Direct interchange with CSX via Meridian & Bigbee line. Creates a new Mexico–Texas–Southeast corridor bypassing Chicago and New Orleans.
  • Houston (UP trackage rights): CPKC operates through the Houston terminal on UP trackage rights and cannot freely serve local Houston traffic.

Section 8 — Major Interchange Gateways: East-West Crossover Points

The Mississippi River corridor contains most of the critical interchange points where western and eastern Class I carriers exchange traffic. These gateways are chokepoints in the U.S. rail network — congestion at any major gateway ripples across the entire system.

Chicago Congestion: Chicago handles ~500 freight trains per day and approximately one-third of all North American rail traffic passes through or originates in the Chicago terminal area. Average dwell time for interchange cars: 24–48 hours. This congestion is a primary driver of the proposed UP-NS merger rationale and ongoing CREATE (Chicago Region Environmental and Transportation Efficiency) improvement program.

Section 9 — Houston as a Rail Hub: Detailed Routing

Houston is one of the most complex and congested rail terminals in North America. Two Class I carriers have significant owned trackage — UP (Englewood Yard) and BNSF — while CPKC operates through the terminal on UP trackage rights. The Port Terminal Railway Association (PTRA) operates within and around the Port of Houston, serving the massive petrochemical industrial complex along the Houston Ship Channel.

PTRA — Port Terminal Railway Association: The PTRA is a switching railroad jointly owned by UP and BNSF. It operates ~25 miles of track within the Port of Houston complex, providing last-mile access to chemical plants and terminal facilities along the Houston Ship Channel. Cars are tendered to/from Class I carriers at Englewood (UP) and Settegast (BNSF) yards.

Section 10 — Practical Routing Notes

Why Just Having Tracks Doesn't Mean Traffic Flows There

  • Precision Scheduled Railroading (PSR): Since 2019, most Class I carriers adopted PSR — point-to-point operations, longer trains, reduced intermediate stops. Traffic has concentrated on fewer, higher-density corridors; many secondary routes see reduced frequency or none at all.
  • Directional Running: On parallel routes (e.g., UP and BNSF Houston–New Orleans), two railroads agree to run trains in only one direction on each line, effectively creating a double-track railroad from two single-track lines. Speeds service and improves reliability, but means trains cannot deviate to the competing line.
  • Trackage Rights vs. Owned Track: A railroad may operate on another's track subject to restrictions on intermediate stops, commodities served, or total volume. CPKC through Houston operates on UP trackage rights — it cannot freely serve Houston local traffic. This distinction is critical when evaluating whether a corridor can actually serve a given origin/destination pair.
  • Abandoned/Downgraded Lines: Many branch lines have been abandoned or downgraded to Class 1–2 track (maximum 25 mph). Only main corridors designated Class 4 or Class 5 (60–79 mph freight speeds) handle through traffic at competitive cycle times.

Coast-to-Coast Routing Today

No single-line transcontinental service exists until UP-NS merger is approved.

  • LA → East Coast via Chicago (Most Common): UP or BNSF to Chicago, interchange to CSX or NS. Adds 24–48 hours of interchange dwell in Chicago.
  • LA → Southeast via Memphis: UP Sunset Route to Houston, northeast to Memphis, interchange to NS or CSX.
  • LA → Southeast via Meridian Speedway: UP to Dallas or Shreveport → NS/CPKC Meridian Speedway to Meridian → then NS to Atlanta. Avoids both New Orleans and Memphis congestion.
  • Houston → Atlanta: UP to New Orleans, interchange to NS Southern Main. Under proposed UP-NS merger, becomes single-line service.
  • Mexico → Southeast: CPKC single-line from Laredo through Meridian, then new interchange to CSX at Myrtlewood, AL.

Section 11 — Moving Coal by Rail

Coal accounts for roughly 15% of Class I railroad revenue. Average transportation cost in 2023: $18.77/short ton. Rail accounts for approximately 69% of all coal deliveries to the electric power sector in the U.S.

Four Producing Regions

  • Powder River Basin (PRB), Wyoming/Montana: Sub-bituminous, ~8,500 BTU/lb, very low sulfur. Largest producing region, 300M+ short tons annually. Accessed exclusively by BNSF and UP via the 103-mile Joint Line (highest-tonnage mainline in the world — triple track, 21 miles of quadruple track over Logan Hill). Peak 2008: 168 loaded/empty coal trains per day. By 2024: ~70 trains combined.
  • Central Appalachia (KY/WV): High-BTU, low-sulfur bituminous. Both CSX and NS compete for traffic. CSX hauls east via Richmond to export terminals at Newport News. NS hauls via Pocahontas Division to Lamberts Point, Norfolk.
  • Northern Appalachia (PA/OH/WV): High-sulfur bituminous. Both CSX and NS serve. Export to Baltimore terminals and Pinney Dock (Lake Erie).
  • Illinois Basin (IL/IN/KY): Higher sulfur but growing production relative to Appalachia. Rail moves to Ohio and Mississippi rivers for barge connections to Gulf Coast export terminals. CN's ex-IC main line provides direct rail access to Gulf.

Carrier-by-Carrier Coal Summary

  • BNSF Largest coal hauler in North America. Primary PRB carrier. Routes south through Colorado to Texas power plants. PRB coal east through Nebraska to Midwest. Pacific export to Westshore Terminals (Roberts Bank, BC) — record 8.2M tonnes in 2011.
  • UP Second-largest coal carrier. PRB via ex-C&NW route east through Nebraska. Also moves Illinois Basin coal. Sunset Route for some Texas export terminal traffic.
  • NS Dominant for Appalachian met/steam coal export. Pocahontas Division direct to Lamberts Point Pier 6 — 48M TPY capacity, largest in the Northern Hemisphere. Coal traffic generated ~two-thirds of freight rail tonnage in Virginia.
  • CSX Appalachian coal to Newport News (Dominion Terminal Associates 22M TPY, Kinder Morgan Pier IX 12M TPY) and Curtis Bay Baltimore. National Gateway double-stack corridor also handles coal unit trains.
  • CN North-south network. Illinois Basin mines through Memphis to New Orleans and Gulf Coast. IC RailMarine Terminal at Convent, LA (rail-to-ship). Also handles Alberta/BC mines to Westshore and Ridley Terminals in Canada.
  • CPKC Modest U.S. coal exposure. On the Canadian side, dominant hauler to Westshore Terminals (~65% of all coal through the facility from SE British Columbia mines).

Hampton Roads: America's Coal Gateway

Hampton Roads — Norfolk and Newport News combined — has been the nation's premier coal export complex since the 1880s. Three active terminals with combined capacity exceeding 80 million tons annually. NS Lamberts Point Pier 6 dumped its billionth ton of coal in 1999 — the only facility in the world to reach that milestone.

The 1,850-foot pier features twin 18-story ship loaders capable of loading two vessels simultaneously at up to 8,000 tons per hour. Storage yards accommodate ~5,200 loaded coal cars (~520,000 tons). Largest single vessel loaded: MV China Prosperity (2019) — 156,779 metric tonnes.

Gulf Coast Coal Export

McDuffie Coal Terminal (Mobile) — 96 acres, 30M TPY capacity, $200M expansion underway. Uniquely served by six railroads plus barge. Lower Mississippi River: United Bulk Terminal at Davant and Kinder Morgan International Marine Terminal at Myrtle Grove. Coal exports from the New Orleans region peaked at 25M short tons in 2012.

Pacific Coast

No active U.S. coal export terminals as of 2025. All Pacific-bound U.S. coal routes through Canadian terminals — Westshore Roberts Bank and Ridley Prince Rupert. Multiple proposed U.S. Pacific terminals (Gateway Pacific, Millennium Bulk) were rejected by Washington and Oregon regulators. U.S. coal for Pacific export must cross an international border.

Unit Train Economics

  • Typical unit train: 100–135 hopper/gondola cars, 3–5 locomotives, ~10,000–16,000 short tons payload, ~1.3 miles long, ~18,000–19,000 gross tons loaded.
  • PRB coal trains: cycle in 5–7 days (Gulf Coast), ~100-hour rotation (Westshore).

Outlook

UP-NS Merger Impact on Coal: If approved, the merger would create the first single-line route from PRB mines to East Coast export terminals — eliminating interchange delays at Chicago, St. Louis, and Memphis that currently add 24–72 hours to total cycle time.

PRB Infrastructure: BNSF and UP have no plans to remove PRB route capacity despite declining domestic coal volumes. Both carriers are positioning the Joint Line for potential growth in export coal and alternative bulk commodity use.

McDuffie Expansion: The $200M McDuffie Terminal expansion underway in Mobile, AL is designed to capture growing Alabama met coal export volumes and position the terminal for the next cycle of global steel demand.
