AUTONOMOUS SHIP AGENCY PLATFORM

Technical Architecture, Market Case & Build Strategy

Date February 2026
Category Garage Project

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report presents the concept, technology architecture, and investment case for an Autonomous Ship Agency Platform — an AI-powered operations system designed to transform how port agent services are delivered worldwide. The platform leverages agentic artificial intelligence to automate the high-volume, time-critical, and data-intensive workflows that define modern ship agency operations.

Ship agency is fundamentally a knowledge brokerage. The agent acts as intermediary between vessel, port authority, terminal, vendor, and principal — coordinating schedules, managing costs, processing thousands of emails daily, and making hundreds of guided decisions per port call. Today, this work is performed almost entirely by humans reading email, making phone calls, and manually editing plain-text documents. The result is chronic delays, coverage gaps, staffing pressure, and error rates that cost the industry millions annually.

The opportunity is to build an intelligent platform that handles the 70% of agency work that is routine and rule-based, surfaces decision-ready data for the 25% that requires guided human judgment, and frees the experienced agent to focus on the 5% that demands true expertise — complex negotiation, relationship management, and problem-solving that no AI can replace.

The core thesis: Ship agency companies today do not even begin to understand where to start with AI. They think it is a product to buy off the shelf. It is not. It is a capability to be built, integrated, and operated — and the first mover who builds it correctly will have a structural advantage that is extremely difficult to replicate.

WHAT IS AGENTIC AI?

1.1 From Chatbots to Autonomous Agents

Agentic AI represents the next evolution beyond the chatbots and generative AI tools that have captured public attention since 2023. While a chatbot waits for a question and provides a response, an agentic AI system can perceive its environment, reason about what needs to happen, take action autonomously, and learn from the outcomes. The distinction is between a tool you use and a system that works alongside you.

MIT Sloan researchers define AI agents as systems that enhance large language models by enabling them to automate complex, multi-step procedures. They can execute plans, use external tools, and interact with digital environments to function as components within larger workflows. The shift from chatbot to agent is the shift from question-and-answer to plan-and-execute.

Key Characteristics of Agentic AI

  • Perception: Ingests and interprets unstructured data — emails, documents, vessel reports, schedule updates — in real time
  • Reasoning: Applies business logic to classify, prioritize, and determine appropriate actions or escalations
  • Action: Executes tasks autonomously — updating databases, sending communications, generating reports, triggering alerts
  • Learning: Improves over time as it processes more data and receives human feedback on its decisions
  • Orchestration: Coordinates multiple specialized sub-agents working in concert across different functions

1.2 The Enterprise Landscape

The momentum behind agentic AI adoption is substantial and accelerating. Gartner predicts that 40% of enterprise applications will include task-specific AI agents by the end of 2026, up from less than 5% in 2025. By 2028, 15% of day-to-day work decisions will be made autonomously through agentic AI. By 2029, at least 50% of knowledge workers will have developed skills to work with, govern, or create AI agents.

However, the reality check is equally important. Deloitte’s 2025 Emerging Technology Trends study found that while 30% of organizations are exploring agentic options and 38% are piloting solutions, only 14% have solutions ready for deployment and just 11% are actively using these systems in production. Furthermore, 42% of organizations report they are still developing their strategy, with 35% having no formal strategy at all. Gartner projects that over 40% of agentic AI projects will fail by 2027 because legacy systems cannot support modern AI execution demands.

This gap between ambition and execution is the opportunity. The maritime industry sits even further behind the curve than the general enterprise landscape. There is no dominant agentic platform for ship agency. The incumbents are selling digitized versions of manual processes — not intelligent, autonomous systems.

1.3 Why “Buy Off the Shelf” Fails

Maritime companies approaching AI adoption overwhelmingly make the same mistake: they assume they can purchase a product that solves the problem. This reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of what agentic AI requires.

  • No commercial off-the-shelf product exists that handles the specific workflows of port ship agency — the email ingestion, schedule management, vendor coordination, cost tracking, and regulatory compliance that defines the work
  • Existing maritime software (MagicPort, ShipIntel, SESCO) digitizes existing workflows but does not automate decision-making or handle unstructured communications
  • Generic enterprise AI platforms (Salesforce Agentforce, Microsoft Copilot) lack domain knowledge of maritime operations, port logistics, and the specific data models that drive agency work
  • The “agent washing” problem is real — Gartner estimates that of thousands of agentic AI vendors, only about 130 offer genuine agentic capabilities. The rest are rebranded chatbots, RPA tools, or assistants

Building an autonomous ship agency platform requires deep domain expertise combined with AI engineering capability. The first mover who combines both will establish a defensible position that generic platforms cannot easily replicate.

THE TECHNOLOGY STACK

2.1 Claude — The Foundation Model

Claude is Anthropic’s flagship large language model and the intelligence layer that powers our platform. Currently valued at $350 billion (January 2026 term sheet), Anthropic is among the most valuable AI companies in the world, backed by Amazon ($8 billion invested), Google ($3.5 billion), and institutional investors including Fidelity, Lightspeed, and the Qatar Investment Authority.

Claude is not a single product — it is a family of models with different capability and cost profiles:

For the autonomous ship agency platform, we use a tiered model strategy: Haiku handles high-volume, simple classification (is this email an ETA update or a crew change request?). Sonnet handles the operational core (extract the data, update the schedule, draft the communication). Opus handles complex scenarios (run the cost analysis for anchorage options, evaluate conflicting schedule constraints). This tiered approach optimizes for both intelligence and cost.

2.2 Claude Code — The Development Engine

Claude Code is Anthropic’s agentic coding assistant, released in February 2025 and made generally available in May 2025. It is a command-line tool that connects to Claude’s models via API and can read files, write code, execute commands, and build complete applications with minimal human direction. Anthropic reported a 5.5x increase in Claude Code revenue by July 2025 and projects over $500 million in annualized revenue from the tool.

Claude Code is critically important for this platform for two reasons:

  • Development velocity: Claude Code enables a small team (or even a single experienced operator with deep domain knowledge) to build production-quality software at a pace that traditionally required a 10-20 person engineering team. The operator provides the domain expertise and architectural vision; Claude Code handles the implementation.
  • Continuous refinement: As the platform encounters new email formats, new port procedures, or new regulatory requirements, Claude Code enables rapid iteration without waiting for a development sprint cycle. This is essential in an industry where every port has unique procedures and every principal has unique reporting requirements.

2.3 Model Context Protocol (MCP) — The Integration Layer

The Model Context Protocol is an open standard introduced by Anthropic in November 2024 that solves the fundamental integration challenge of agentic AI: connecting the intelligent model to the data and systems it needs to be useful. MCP has been adopted by OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, and was donated to the Linux Foundation’s Agentic AI Foundation in December 2025.

Before MCP, connecting an AI agent to a business system required building custom integration code for every combination of model and system — an N\u00d7M problem that made scaling prohibitively expensive. MCP provides a universal protocol: build the connection once, and any MCP-compatible agent can use it.

For the autonomous ship agency platform, MCP is the bridge to existing systems. The agent can connect to email servers (Exchange, Gmail), accounting systems, terminal operating systems, port community systems, and vessel tracking APIs through standardized MCP connectors. This means the platform does not require ripping out existing infrastructure — it sits on top of it, reading from and writing to the systems already in place.

2.4 Claude Cowork — The Non-Technical Interface

Claude Cowork, released in January 2026, extends Claude’s capabilities from the command line to a graphical interface aimed at non-technical users. It enables office workers to automate file management, data processing, and routine tasks without writing code. For the ship agency platform, Cowork represents the pathway for operations staff to interact with and customize the platform without requiring engineering support.

THE SHIP AGENCY PROBLEM

3.1 What a Ship Agent Actually Does

A ship agent is a knowledge broker and operational intermediary. When a vessel is scheduled to call at a port, the agent coordinates every aspect of that port call between the vessel, the port authority, the terminal operator, vendors, government agencies, and the vessel’s principal (the entity paying for the service).

The core data object is the Vessel Port Call — a record that tracks: the vessel’s estimated time of arrival (ETA), actual arrival, berth assignment, cargo operations timeline, departure time, and ETA to the next port. Every other agency function connects to this record.

Daily Operational Reality

  • 5,000–6,000 emails per day from vessels, terminals, principals, vendors, port authorities, and government agencies — all manually read, interpreted, and acted upon
  • Port schedule management: Monitoring vessel traffic at the designated port facility, berth rotation, anchorage assignments, and updating all stakeholders when parameters change
  • Vendor coordination: Crew changes, spare parts delivery, bunker supply, provisions, surveys, inspections — each requiring PO generation, scheduling, confirmation, and invoicing
  • Cost tracking: Dockage ($50K+/day at some terminals), pilotage, towage, line handling, cargo handling, agency fees — all tracked against a funding allotment from the principal
  • Regulatory compliance: Customs entry, immigration, Coast Guard documentation, environmental compliance, hazardous cargo notifications
  • Reporting: Statement of Facts (SOF), disbursement accounts, time sheets, arrival/departure notices — each formatted to principal-specific requirements

PLATFORM ARCHITECTURE

4.1 Core Design Principles

  • Vessel Port Call as the central data object: Every function, alert, report, and decision connects back to the port call record. This is the single source of truth.
  • Human-in-the-loop, not human-out-of-the-loop: The platform handles volume and routine; the human handles judgment and relationships. The human operator sits at a dashboard overseeing key decisions.
  • Confidence-based autonomy: The system classifies its own confidence in each action. High confidence = execute autonomously. Medium confidence = present recommendation for approval. Low confidence = escalate to human with full context.
  • Bridge existing systems, don’t replace them: MCP connectors integrate with existing email, accounting, and terminal systems. This is not a rip-and-replace — it is an intelligence layer on top of existing infrastructure.
  • Progressive automation: Start with the highest-volume, lowest-risk functions (email classification, schedule distribution) and progressively expand to higher-judgment functions (cost optimization, vendor management).

4.2 Functional Modules

Module 1: Email Intelligence Engine

The intake pipe. Processes 5,000+ daily emails through a five-stage pipeline: Inbound \u2192 Classify \u2192 Extract \u2192 Route \u2192 Act. The classification layer identifies email type (ETA update, crew change request, invoice, schedule alert, routine confirmation) with 95%+ accuracy. The extraction layer pulls structured data (dates, times, vessel names, costs) from unstructured text. The routing layer determines whether the action can be taken autonomously or requires human approval.

Module 2: Schedule State Engine

The operational core. Maintains the real-time state of every vessel port call: ETA, arrival, berthing, working, completion, departure, ETA next port. When the Email Intelligence Engine detects a schedule change, the Schedule State Engine updates the record, evaluates the impact on other vessels and berth rotation, and triggers the appropriate distribution. If the ETA is distant and the change is minor, it sends autonomously. If the change is dramatic (e.g., 3-day delay), it alerts the human operator at 2 AM for confirmation rather than waiting 5–8 hours.

Module 3: Alert & Distribution System

The communication layer. Compiles schedule updates into the format required by each recipient — some principals want a one-line email, others want a detailed report with all port times. The system maintains recipient profiles and distribution templates. It handles the array of information needs from a single data source: the port call record.

Module 4: Cost Tracking & Budget Alerts

The financial control layer. Tracks port call expenses in real time against the funding allotment. If dockage is $50K/day and the vessel enters day 4 of an expected 2-day stay, the system fires an alert immediately — not when the invoice arrives weeks later. It tracks pilotage, towage, line handling, cargo handling, and all other disbursement items against budgets and flags variances.

Module 5: Decision Support & Scenario Analysis

The strategic layer. When the human operator faces a routing decision (anchorage A vs. B vs. C), the system pulls the cost parameters (tug rates, transit distances, berth availability, bunkering requirements) and runs the permutations in real time. Example: If the vessel needs to bunker, Anchorage A requires one river turn ($85K total) while Anchorage B requires two turns ($135K). If no bunkering is needed, Anchorage C is lowest cost at $72K. This analysis, which would take a human 30 minutes and multiple phone calls, is surfaced via app in under 60 seconds.

Module 6: Vendor & Crew Management

The coordination layer. When a crewing manager sends a crew change request by email, the agent reads the email, confirms the request, identifies the appropriate vendor, generates the purchase order, schedules the delivery, and compiles the details required for invoicing after service completion. The human reviews the PO before it is sent; the system handles everything else.

Module 7: SOF & Invoicing Automation

The accounting layer. Port times from the Schedule State Engine feed directly into Statement of Facts compilation. The SOF is auto-generated per principal format requirements. Time sheets and disbursement accounts are compiled from real-time data rather than reconstructed from memory and emails after the fact. Incoming invoices are ingested electronically, matched to vessel files and POs, routed through approval workflows, and assigned to vessel cost files.

Module 8: Client Portal & App

The access layer. Principals and key stakeholders can interact with the agency via a web or mobile app. Real-time vessel status, schedule updates, cost tracking, and document access — available on demand rather than waiting for the agent to compile and email a report. When the human agent needs to approve a decision, they do so via the app — review the data, tap approve or modify, and the system executes.

HUMAN-IN-THE-LOOP DESIGN

The platform is explicitly designed around the principle that there is no replacement for the human ship agent overseeing the port call. The goal is not to eliminate the human — it is to eliminate the 70% of the human’s time that is consumed by reading emails, transposing data, and performing routine tasks, so that the human can focus on the work that actually requires human judgment.

The escalation model works like a traditional customer service ticketing system. The AI agent handles what it can; when it encounters a situation outside its confidence threshold, it escalates to the live human operator with all relevant context pre-assembled. The human never starts from zero — they receive a brief with the situation, the options, the data, and a recommended action.

LEVERAGING EXISTING SYSTEMS

A critical design principle is that this platform bridges development gaps rather than requiring wholesale system replacement. Ship agencies operate on a patchwork of email clients, spreadsheets, legacy accounting software, and industry-specific tools. The platform integrates with this ecosystem through MCP connectors and API bridges.

Email Systems (Exchange, Gmail, IMAP)

The Email Intelligence Engine connects directly to existing email infrastructure. It reads the inbox the same way the human agent does — it just does it 24/7, in under 30 seconds, with perfect recall and no fatigue.

Accounting & ERP Systems

Disbursement data, invoice matching, and expense tracking feed into existing accounting workflows. The platform does not replace the accounting system — it populates it accurately and in real time rather than through manual data entry weeks after the port call.

Terminal Operating Systems

Where terminals expose schedule data via APIs or port community systems, the platform ingests it directly. Where they do not, the Email Intelligence Engine captures the same information from the emails the terminal sends.

Vessel Tracking (AIS)

Real-time vessel position data from AIS feeds directly into ETA calculations and schedule state management. When a vessel’s speed or routing changes, the system recalculates ETA automatically.

SaaS Transitions

For agencies looking to transition from legacy on-premise systems to cloud-based operations, the platform provides a natural migration path. The AI layer can read from the old system and write to the new one, serving as a bridge during transition periods. This is significantly less disruptive than a hard cutover.

TRAINING, STAFF DEVELOPMENT & QUALITY OF LIFE

The platform directly addresses the maritime industry’s talent crisis. Ship agency operations require 24/7 coverage, involve high-stress time-sensitive decisions, and have historically offered limited work-life balance. The result is difficulty attracting young talent and high turnover among experienced staff.

7.1 How the Platform Changes the Work

  • Entry-level staff are freed from email triage and data entry. Instead of spending their first year reading thousands of emails to learn the business, they interact with an AI system that surfaces the important information and explains the context. The learning curve accelerates dramatically.
  • Mid-level operators shift from reactive firefighting to proactive oversight. They manage by exception rather than by brute-force email processing. The AI agents may be more reliable than entry or mid-level employees at handling routine, time-consuming tasks because they can maintain focus and consistency indefinitely.
  • Senior agents focus on the work that leverages their decades of experience: complex negotiations, relationship management, problem-solving for unusual situations, and strategic decision-making. Their expertise is amplified, not replaced.

7.2 Knowledge Preservation

When an experienced agent retires or leaves, their institutional knowledge leaves with them. The platform captures operational procedures, decision patterns, vendor relationships, and port-specific knowledge in a structured format that persists regardless of staff turnover. The AI learns from the decisions the experienced agents make and carries that knowledge forward.

7.3 Recruiting Advantage

The company that deploys this platform can offer a fundamentally different value proposition to talent: modern technology, reduced routine workload, genuine work-life balance (the AI handles the 2 AM alerts), and career development focused on high-value skills rather than data entry. In an industry struggling to attract young professionals, this is a significant competitive advantage.

THE COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

The maritime software market was valued at $1.86 billion in 2025 and is projected to reach $3.38 billion by 2033 (7.6% CAGR). However, no existing player offers a true agentic AI platform for ship agency operations.

The white space is clear: No one is building an AI-native, agentic platform specifically for ship agency operations. The companies in this space are either digitizing manual processes or building for vessel operations, not the shore-side knowledge brokerage that is ship agency.

BUILD APPROACH & PHASING

The platform is designed as a modular microservices architecture. Each module operates independently, connects to the central vessel port call record, and can be deployed, tested, and refined without disrupting other modules. This enables a phased rollout that proves value at each stage.
