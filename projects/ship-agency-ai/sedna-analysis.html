A Critical Analysis for Ship Agency Leadership

Is purpose-built maritime email software still the answer, or has AI made it possible to solve this problem internally?

Confidential Working Paper — February 2026, Revised Edition

1. Executive Summary

This paper examines Sedna, the dominant maritime email management platform, through the lens of a rapidly changing AI technology landscape and an increasingly revealing financial picture. The central question facing ship agency leadership is deceptively simple: Do we still need Sedna, or can we now solve this problem ourselves?

The answer is nuanced. Sedna solved a real and serious problem when it launched in 2017. Ship agents processing 500 to 1,500 emails per day were drowning in Outlook's limitations: no shared visibility, no auto-tagging, no cross-team searchability, and no way to tie an email thread to a vessel, voyage, or job number without manual filing. Sedna wrapped around Microsoft Exchange/Office 365 infrastructure and provided a purpose-built collaboration layer that Outlook was never designed to offer.

However, four fundamental shifts have occurred since Sedna's founding that warrant serious re-evaluation:

  • Microsoft 365 Copilot and Power Automate now deliver many of the core capabilities that once required a third-party wrapper, including AI-powered email summarization, shared mailbox intelligence, auto-classification, and workflow automation.
  • Large Language Models (LLMs) have matured to a point where custom AI agents can parse, classify, route, and summarize maritime email at a fraction of the cost and complexity of maintaining a dedicated SaaS platform.
  • Sedna's own business trajectory raises strategic questions: $86 million in equity funding plus €10 million in debt against estimated revenues of $2–4 million, an acquisition spree that appears focused on buying customer books rather than technology, and a pivot from email management to "The OS for Shipping" that puts it in direct competition with established maritime software providers.
  • The broader maritime platform ecosystem is consolidating around integration rather than ownership of the email layer. Marcura, with $326 million in revenue and over 1,000 employees, chose to partner with Sedna rather than build or acquire its own email capability—a strategic signal that the email management layer may be viewed as a feature rather than a platform.

This paper does not advocate for or against Sedna. It provides a neutral, evidence-based framework for ship agency executives to evaluate their options, understand the risks of each path, and make a decision informed by technology reality, financial analysis, and market dynamics rather than vendor marketing or inertia.

2. The Problem: Why Ship Agencies Need Email Intelligence

Before evaluating solutions, leadership must understand the problem clearly. A typical ship agent's inbox is not like a typical corporate inbox. The volume, velocity, and variety of maritime email creates operational challenges that generic email clients were never designed to handle.

2.1 The Volume Problem

A single port call can generate 50 to 200 emails across arrival notices, customs declarations, berth requests, cargo documentation, crew lists, immigration forms, surveyor correspondence, principal instructions, and invoice reconciliation. A busy ship agency handling 10 to 20 port calls simultaneously may see individual agents processing 500 to 1,500 emails per day. These are not newsletters or CC chains—each email may contain actionable instructions, deadline-sensitive documents, or regulatory compliance requirements.

2.2 The Visibility Problem

In a standard Outlook environment, email lives in individual mailboxes. When Agent A goes on leave, Agent B has no visibility into the 300 emails Agent A processed on a vessel that is still in port. The result is repeated requests to counterparties, missed instructions, and operational errors that cost money. Shared mailboxes in Outlook partially solve this but introduce their own chaos: everyone sees everything, there is no intelligent routing, and the filing burden actually increases.

2.3 The Search and Retrieval Problem

Maritime operations are auditable. When a principal disputes a disbursement, when customs questions a declaration, when a P&I club investigates an incident, the agency must produce email correspondence quickly and completely. Outlook's search has historically been slow, unreliable across large mailboxes, and incapable of cross-user search without Exchange eDiscovery tools that most ship agencies do not have configured.

2.4 The Context Problem

The most costly inefficiency is context switching. An agent receives an email about MV Pacific Star's berth change. To act on it, they must cross-reference the vessel's current proforma, the principal's standing instructions, the port's berth allocation schedule, and the customs timeline. In Outlook, this means switching between folders, spreadsheets, port software, and agency management systems. In Sedna, this context is surfaced alongside the email through integrations and tagging.

These four problems are real, persistent, and directly impact operational efficiency and profitability. Any solution—whether Sedna, an internal build, or a hybrid approach—must address all four.

3. Sedna: Product, Company, and Strategic Analysis

3.1 What Sedna Actually Does

At its core, Sedna is an email middleware layer. It connects to a company's existing Microsoft 365 (or Google Workspace) email infrastructure via API, ingests all incoming and outgoing mail, and presents it through Sedna's own web and mobile interface. The underlying email infrastructure remains Microsoft—Sedna does not replace Exchange or Outlook. It wraps around them.

Key functional capabilities include:

  • Shared Team Inboxes: All team members see all relevant mail without forwarding. Emails can be followed, commented on internally, and tagged without cluttering the external thread.
  • Auto-Tagging and Job Classification: Incoming emails are automatically tagged by vessel name, voyage number, or job reference using rule-based pattern matching and, more recently, AI classification.
  • Rapid Search: Sedna's search indexes millions of messages and returns results in under one second with boolean precision—far outperforming Outlook's native search.
  • Activity Panel: Internal comments, tasks, and notes are attached to individual emails without generating additional email traffic. This is effectively a Slack-like sidebar embedded in each message.
  • Integration Layer: Sedna connects with maritime-specific systems including Veson IMOS, Q88, Abraxa, Softmar, GAT-Ship, and Chain.io (CargoWise), pulling contextual data into the email view.
  • Compliance and Archiving: All emails are immutably archived and searchable, supporting audit and regulatory requirements.

3.5 The Marcura Partnership: A Market Signal

In May 2025, Marcura and Sedna announced a formal integration partnership. This relationship provides an important lens for evaluating Sedna's market position and the broader question of whether maritime email management is a platform or a feature.

Marcura is a Dubai-headquartered maritime technology company backed by Marlin Equity Partners ($8.9 billion AUM) with over $326 million in annual revenue and 1,000+ employees. Its platform stack includes DA-Desk (port cost management), PortLog (voyage optimization), MarTrust (maritime payments), ShipServ (procurement), and ClaimsHub (claims processing). Marcura handles over 200,000 port calls and $21.8 billion in maritime payments annually, serving vessels that transport roughly one-third of global seaborne commodities.

The Sedna-Marcura integration gives chartering and operations teams access to Marcura's ClaimsHub and AI Console directly within Sedna's Stream email interface. A DA-Desk integration for inline PDA and FDA approvals followed in February 2026, and PortLog integration is planned for the next phase.

Why this matters for the analysis: Marcura is the most acquisitive maritime platform consolidator in the market, having bought ShipServ (2023), VesselMan (January 2025), HubSE (February 2025), Shipster (March 2025), and Brightwell (July 2025). It has the capital, engineering resources, and strategic appetite to build or acquire almost anything in the maritime technology stack. Yet Marcura chose to partner with Sedna rather than build or buy its own email layer.

This choice is revealing. It suggests that Marcura views email management as a complementary feature to its core platform—not a standalone product category worth owning. The integration approach lets Marcura offer email-adjacent capabilities to shared customers without the cost and complexity of maintaining an email platform. For Sedna, the partnership validates its product as the de facto maritime email layer but also implicitly confirms that the email management function is becoming a connector between more valuable maritime operations systems, rather than a destination platform in its own right.

For ship agency leadership, the takeaway is straightforward: even the largest and most well-resourced maritime platform company in the world does not believe it needs to own the email management layer. The corollary is that neither does your organization—as long as the email layer connects effectively to the systems that matter.

4. The Investor Room: What Sedna Must Show to Survive

This section steps outside the customer perspective and examines Sedna from the viewpoint of its investors, particularly Insight Partners. Understanding the financial dynamics that drive Sedna's strategic decisions helps customers assess platform risk.

4.1 What Insight Partners Needs

Insight Partners' playbook for vertical SaaS is well-documented: they invest in companies that can reach $100M+ in annual recurring revenue, dominate a niche category, and compound at 30–40% annually toward an IPO or strategic exit. Insight led both the $34 million Series B and $42 million Series C, meaning they have approximately $76 million deployed into Sedna. Their entry valuations likely ranged from $150–300 million, implying they need a $750 million to $1.5 billion exit to achieve their target returns.

At the current revenue trajectory ($2–4 million ARR against $97 million in total capital), Sedna has generated roughly 2–4 cents of revenue per dollar invested. This is significantly below typical benchmarks for a Series C company, where investors expect $10–20 million ARR and a clear path to $50 million.

4.3 Market Size Constraints

The maritime software market provides a finite addressable universe:

  • Global maritime software market: approximately $1.8–$4.5 billion (2025), growing at 7–15% CAGR depending on which segment.
  • Maritime email and communications management: estimated $90–180 million addressable, representing perhaps 5–10% of total maritime software spend.
  • Global addressable organizations: approximately 5,000–8,000 maritime companies with sufficient email volume to justify a dedicated platform (ship agencies, charterers, operators, commodity traders, major brokers).
  • Total addressable seats: at 50–100 users per organization, the global maritime email TAM is approximately 250,000–800,000 seats.
  • Sedna's current position: an estimated 15,000–25,000 seats across 500+ customers represents roughly 3–10% market penetration.

The TAM ceiling: Even at full market saturation, the maritime email management market does not support a $1 billion revenue company. This is precisely why Sedna is pivoting to "The OS for Shipping"—the email-only TAM is too small to justify the capital invested. By expanding into voyage management (Dataloy), AI-powered document processing (Flytta), and claims integration (Marcura partnership), Sedna is attempting to expand its TAM to $500 million or more. Whether it can credibly compete in these broader categories against established players with decades of market presence is the central strategic risk.

5. Technical Architecture: What You Are Actually Buying

5.1 Sedna's Technology Stack

Based on publicly available data (job postings, technology profile databases, and integration documentation), Sedna's stack includes:

  • Backend: Node.js with TypeScript
  • Search: OpenSearch (an open-source Elasticsearch fork)
  • Frontend: Modern web application (React-based, per job listings)
  • Infrastructure: Cloud-native, likely AWS or Azure
  • Email Connectivity: Microsoft Exchange Web Services (EWS) and Graph API for O365 integration
  • Security: ISO certified, GDPR and CPA compliant, corporate MFA integration

Key insight: None of these technologies are proprietary. Sedna's competitive advantage is not in its technology stack—it is in its maritime domain knowledge, its pre-built integrations with shipping-specific systems, and its seven years of customer-driven feature refinement. This distinction matters because it tells you exactly what you would need to replicate (the domain layer) versus what is readily available as commodity technology (the email processing, search, and AI classification layers).

5.2 The O365 Wrapper Architecture

Sedna does not replace your email server. Your Microsoft 365 licenses, Exchange Online mailboxes, and Outlook installations all remain in place. Sedna connects via API and creates a parallel view of your email with additional intelligence layered on top. This means:

  • You are paying for Microsoft 365 AND Sedna simultaneously.
  • If Sedna experiences an outage, your email still functions through Outlook.
  • If you leave Sedna, your email history remains in Microsoft 365 (though Sedna-specific metadata like tags, comments, and activity panel data would be lost unless exported).
  • Sedna's value is entirely in the intelligence, collaboration, and workflow layer—not in email transport or storage.

This architecture is important for the migration discussion later in this paper. It means transitioning off Sedna is technically straightforward (email never leaves Microsoft), but operationally complex (workflow habits, tag taxonomies, and institutional knowledge embedded in Sedna's collaboration features must be replicated elsewhere).

6. The Microsoft 365 Alternative: What Can Be Done Internally Now

The most significant development since Sedna's founding is the rapid advancement of Microsoft's own AI and automation capabilities within the 365 ecosystem. This section examines what is possible today using tools that most ship agencies already have licenses for.

6.1 Microsoft 365 Copilot in Outlook

As of late 2025, Microsoft 365 Copilot delivers the following capabilities directly within Outlook:

  • Email Summarization: Copilot condenses lengthy email threads into concise summaries, highlighting decisions, action items, and key information.
  • Shared Mailbox Intelligence: Copilot now works within shared mailboxes, allowing teams to use AI to manage shared inboxes collaboratively. This directly addresses the visibility problem.
  • Draft Generation: Context-aware reply drafting that cites relevant attachments and prior correspondence.
  • Search Enhancement: Copilot-powered search spans email, Teams messages, and OneDrive/SharePoint documents in a single query.
  • Attachment Processing: Copilot reads Word, Excel, PDF, JSON, and XML attachments and includes that information in responses.

6.2 Power Automate and Copilot Studio

Microsoft's low-code/no-code automation platform can now replicate several of Sedna's core features:

  • Email Classification Agents: Autonomous AI agents in Copilot Studio can monitor shared mailboxes, read incoming email content, identify intent (vessel arrival, cargo documentation, customs query, invoice, etc.), and route messages to appropriate teams or folders.
  • Auto-Tagging: Power Automate flows can extract vessel names, IMO numbers, voyage references, and job numbers from email content and apply labels, move to folders, or update SharePoint lists.
  • SharePoint Document Management: Attachments can be automatically saved to structured SharePoint libraries organized by vessel, voyage, or port call.
  • CRM/ERP Integration: Copilot agents can connect to Dynamics 365, SharePoint, and third-party systems to fetch and display contextual data.
  • Automated Responses: For routine queries (ETA requests, standard disbursement acknowledgments), AI agents can draft and send responses with human oversight.

6.3 What Microsoft 365 Cannot Yet Do (Sedna's Remaining Moat)

An honest assessment must acknowledge where Sedna still has clear advantages over a native Microsoft 365 approach:

  • Pre-Built Maritime Integrations: Sedna's plug-and-play connections with Veson IMOS, Q88, Abraxa, Softmar, GAT-Ship, Chain.io/CargoWise, and now Marcura's ClaimsHub/DA-Desk/PortLog are genuinely valuable. Replicating these integrations internally requires API development and ongoing maintenance.
  • Seven Years of Maritime UX Refinement: Sedna's interface was designed by people who understand shipping workflows. The tag taxonomy, the job reference system, the activity panel UX—these were built through thousands of hours of feedback from ship operators, charterers, and agents.
  • Immutable Email Archive: While Microsoft 365 offers litigation hold and retention policies, Sedna's approach to email immutability and the inability to delete emails is a simpler compliance story for maritime operations.
  • Cross-Organization Email Visibility: Sedna provides a single pane of glass across the entire organization's email, which is more seamless than configuring shared mailbox access and permissions in Exchange Online.

8. Transitioning Off Sedna: A Practical Migration Framework

If leadership decides to move away from Sedna, the transition must be planned carefully. The following framework minimizes operational disruption.

8.1 Phase 1: Data Inventory and Export (Weeks 1–4)

  • Export all Sedna tags, job references, and classification taxonomies into a structured spreadsheet.
  • Export activity panel comments and internal notes (if Sedna provides an export API; verify with Sedna support).
  • Document all active Sedna integrations (Veson, Q88, Marcura, etc.) and identify which will need alternative connections.
  • Confirm that all historical email is accessible through Microsoft 365 (it should be, since Sedna wraps around Exchange).

8.2 Phase 2: Build the Replacement Layer (Weeks 2–8)

  • Configure shared mailboxes in Exchange Online with appropriate permissions.
  • Deploy Microsoft 365 Copilot to all operational users.
  • Build Power Automate flows for email classification, auto-tagging, and document routing.
  • Create SharePoint libraries structured by vessel, voyage, and job reference.
  • Configure Microsoft Teams channels as the replacement for Sedna's Activity Panel (internal comments per vessel/job).
  • If custom AI agents are desired, begin Azure OpenAI integration for maritime-specific email parsing.

8.3 Phase 3: Parallel Running (Weeks 6–12)

  • Run both Sedna and the new system simultaneously.
  • Assign 2–3 power users to each system to validate that all use cases are covered.
  • Document gaps—any workflow that works in Sedna but does not yet work in the replacement.
  • Address gaps through additional Power Automate flows or custom development.

8.4 Phase 4: Cutover (Weeks 12–16)

  • Migrate all users to the new system.
  • Maintain Sedna in read-only mode for 90 days for historical reference.
  • Terminate Sedna licenses at contract renewal.

Risk mitigation: The biggest risk in this transition is not technical—it is behavioral. Users who have spent years in Sedna will resist change. Executive sponsorship, clear communication about the reasons for the change, and adequate training are essential. Budget 2–4 weeks of productivity dip during transition.

9. Will Products Like Sedna Survive the AI Era?

This is the strategic question at the heart of this analysis. To answer it fairly, we must consider both sides.

9.1 The Case That Sedna Survives and Thrives

  • Maritime is a conservative industry. Adoption cycles are long. Many agencies will not build internally because they lack IT capability or appetite for technology risk. Sedna's value proposition as a managed, turnkey solution remains compelling for these organizations.
  • Sedna is evolving. The Dataloy acquisition gives Sedna a voyage management system. The Flytta acquisition gives it AI capability. The Marcura partnership connects it to the largest maritime back-office platform in the world. If Sedna successfully integrates these assets, it becomes a more defensible, higher-value platform than a simple email wrapper.
  • Domain expertise matters. Microsoft Copilot is a general-purpose AI. It does not know what a Notice of Readiness is, what a Statement of Facts looks like, or how to parse a Proforma Disbursement Account. Sedna's maritime-specific AI (once fully deployed) could maintain a meaningful quality advantage for domain-specific tasks.
  • Switching costs are real. Agencies that have years of tag taxonomies, workflow habits, and institutional knowledge embedded in Sedna face genuine friction in leaving.

9.2 The Case That Sedna Becomes Obsolete

  • The wrapper business model is inherently fragile. When the platform you wrap around (Microsoft 365) develops the same capabilities natively, the wrapper's value proposition erodes. Microsoft's 2025 Copilot updates (shared mailbox AI, email attachment processing, cross-app search) directly attack Sedna's core feature set.
  • AI commoditization is accelerating. The ability to classify, tag, route, and summarize emails is no longer a differentiator—it is a commodity capability available through Azure OpenAI, Google Cloud AI, or even open-source models. What was a competitive moat in 2019 is table stakes in 2026.
  • Revenue-to-funding disparity. At $2–4 million in revenue against $97 million in capital deployed, Sedna needs 15–30x revenue growth to justify the capital invested. If this does not materialize, investors may push for cost cuts, acquisition, or wind-down, creating platform risk for customers.
  • Acquisition integration risk. Integrating four acquisitions in less than twelve months (Nordic IT, CompassAir, Flytta, Dataloy) with approximately 20 engineers is operationally challenging. History shows that rapid acquisition strategies in niche SaaS often result in fragmented products, cultural integration challenges, and distracted engineering teams.
  • The "OS for Shipping" positioning competes with established players. By moving into voyage management, Sedna now competes directly with Veson IMOS, Q88, and other providers that have deeper market penetration and longer customer relationships. Marcura itself, through its Dataloy partnership (announced June 2025), is building the same integration from the Dataloy side—meaning the voyage management layer connects to maritime back-office with or without Sedna.
  • The Marcura precedent. The world's largest maritime platform company evaluated the email management problem and concluded that partnership (not ownership) was the right approach. This implicitly values the email layer as a connector, not a platform—and connectors are historically vulnerable to commoditization.

9.3 The Most Likely Outcome

Based on current trajectory, the most probable scenario is that Sedna persists as a viable product for the next 3–5 years but faces increasing pressure on three fronts: pricing pressure as Microsoft native capabilities improve, competitive pressure as its "OS for Shipping" strategy brings it into conflict with established maritime software providers, and economic pressure from the gap between its cost base and revenue.

The acqui-customer strategy (buying Nordic IT's 400+ customers, Dataloy's 500+ customers) provides Sedna with a meaningful runway of cross-sell and upsell opportunity. But converting legacy customers on low-cost contracts to Sedna's premium pricing will be the make-or-break execution challenge of 2026–2027.

Ship agency leadership should not expect Sedna to disappear overnight, but should actively reduce single-vendor dependency and ensure their email infrastructure can function independently of Sedna at any point.

10. Alternative SaaS Platforms

If the question is specifically "what else could we buy instead of Sedna?" the options are limited in the maritime-specific space but broader in the general team email management category.

10.1 Maritime-Specific Alternatives

  • Dualog (now Voyager Portal): Norwegian maritime communications platform focused on ship-to-shore email management. More vessel-focused than agency-focused.
  • CompassAir: Was an independent maritime email workflow tool before Sedna acquired it in 2025. No longer an independent option.
  • Shipfix: Focuses on maritime email parsing and trade intelligence. Addresses a narrower slice of the problem (commodity trade email parsing) rather than full email management.
  • Nordic IT reMARK: Was the primary Sedna alternative before being acquired in November 2024. No longer an independent option. The acquisition of both CompassAir and Nordic IT leaves Sedna with no direct maritime-specific email management competitor—a fact that should concern customers negotiating pricing.

10.2 General Team Email Management

  • Front (front.com): Shared inbox platform with AI capabilities, workflow automation, and CRM integrations. Not maritime-specific, but highly configurable. Pricing: $19–$229/user/month depending on tier.
  • Hiver: Shared inbox built on top of Gmail/Outlook. Simpler and cheaper than Sedna but lacks maritime integrations. Pricing: $19–$49/user/month.
  • Missive: Collaborative email platform with team inboxes, internal chat, and task management. Pricing: $14–$26/user/month.

None of these general platforms offer Sedna's maritime-specific integrations or domain expertise. However, they demonstrate that the core technology of shared inbox management, email tagging, and team collaboration is widely available at significantly lower price points. The "maritime-specific" premium Sedna commands should be evaluated against the actual cost of configuring a general platform for maritime use—particularly given that Sedna's acquisition of both major maritime email competitors has eliminated alternatives, potentially reducing competitive pricing pressure.

11. Decision Framework for Ship Agency Leadership

The following framework helps leadership make a structured decision based on their organization's specific situation.

11.1 Stay with Sedna If:

  • Your organization lacks internal IT capability to build and maintain custom solutions.
  • You rely heavily on Sedna's pre-built integrations with Veson, Q88, Marcura, or other maritime systems.
  • Your team has deep institutional knowledge embedded in Sedna's tag structure and workflows.
  • You are willing to accept the platform risk in exchange for operational simplicity.
  • Your contract terms are favorable and pricing has been negotiated below list rates.

11.2 Move to Microsoft 365 + Copilot If:

  • You already have Microsoft 365 E3 or E5 licenses deployed.
  • Your organization has even basic IT capability (or access to a Microsoft partner) for configuration.
  • You want the lowest ongoing cost of ownership.
  • You are comfortable with a solution that covers 70–80% of Sedna's functionality natively, with the remaining gap addressable through Power Automate.
  • You value strategic independence from niche SaaS vendors.

11.3 Build a Custom AI Solution If:

  • You view email intelligence as a competitive advantage worth investing in.
  • You have access to development resources (internal or contracted).
  • You want 100% control over your data, workflows, and AI behavior.
  • You are willing to accept higher Year 1 costs for lower long-term costs and greater flexibility.
  • You anticipate that AI capabilities will continue to improve rapidly, and you want to ride that curve rather than be locked into a vendor's development roadmap.

12. Conclusion

Sedna solved a real problem at a time when the tools to solve it internally did not exist. In 2017, when Microsoft Outlook was the only interface to Exchange email and AI-powered email classification was science fiction, a purpose-built maritime email platform was the only viable answer to the 1,000-emails-a-day problem.

That is no longer the case. The technology landscape has shifted fundamentally. Microsoft 365 Copilot, Power Automate, Azure OpenAI, and the broader explosion of AI capabilities now provide ship agencies with the building blocks to address the same problems internally—at lower cost, with less vendor dependency, and with a technology trajectory that is accelerating in their favor.

The financial picture adds urgency to this assessment. Sedna has consumed approximately $97 million in capital to build a company generating $2–4 million in revenue. Its acquisition strategy—buying customer books from legacy providers like Nordic IT, absorbing competitors like CompassAir, and expanding into voyage management through Dataloy—is a rational response to the constraints of its email-only TAM, but it introduces significant execution risk. The company must simultaneously integrate four acquisitions, migrate legacy customers to premium pricing, defend against Microsoft's native AI improvements, and compete with established players in new categories. Even the Marcura partnership, while validating Sedna's product, implicitly confirms that the maritime industry's largest platform company views email management as a feature rather than a platform worth owning.

This does not mean Sedna is worthless. For organizations with limited IT capability, heavy reliance on maritime-specific integrations, or deep operational dependency on Sedna's workflows, the platform continues to deliver value. The convenience premium is real.

But leadership should understand what they are paying for: not proprietary technology, but a pre-assembled collection of capabilities that are increasingly available as commodity components. The "convenience tax" is approximately $140,000 over three years for a 50-user agency, over and above what a Microsoft-native solution would cost. Whether that premium is justified depends entirely on your organization's IT maturity, integration requirements, and appetite for change.

The strongest strategic position for any ship agency in 2026 is this: use whatever tools work today, but ensure that your email, data, and workflows are never locked into a single platform whose long-term viability depends on a financial trajectory that has not yet materialized. Whether you stay with Sedna, move to Microsoft native tools, or build a custom solution, the underlying principle is the same—own your data, control your workflows, and maintain the ability to switch.
