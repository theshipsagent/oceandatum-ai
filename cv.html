Professional Biography & Work History

Origins

Born and raised beside the Atlantic in Virginia Beach, I grew up watching ships pass Cape Henry—sometimes finding ship's mail or chunks of coal washed ashore from passing vessels while playing in the surf. I am a second-generation maritime professional; my earliest memory of boarding a commercial vessel was in 1979 aboard the Export Builder, when my father was an agent for American Export Isbrandtsen Lines. He would go on to a worldwide career, following American Export through Farrell Lines, then transitioning to commercial and leadership roles for Wilhelmsen Lines in 1989 with global responsibilities, until the final corporate merger of Wallenius and Wilhelmsen in 2017, where he began work for COSCO until retirement.

The ocean and my father's trinkets from overseas forged a lifelong interest in maritime commerce. The smell of salt water, the sight of ships on the horizon, and stories of distant ports shaped my professional aspirations from an early age.

My first commercial work came at age ten, selling stationery for prizes. At thirteen, I began working summers at a bicycle shop—a formative experience in commercial business built around mechanical assets that would prove beneficial later in life. I continued that job through high school graduation, working over forty hours weekly while attending school during my senior year. I have been gainfully employed for nearly forty years, with only four employers in that span.

Military Service

After graduating from First Colonial High School in Virginia Beach, I took a different path than most peers and enlisted in the United States Army. I served as an infantryman assigned to a military intelligence long-range surveillance unit in the 101st Airborne Division at Fort Campbell, Kentucky (1993–1995). The discipline, attention to detail, and crisis management skills developed during military service would prove more valuable throughout my maritime career than any college degree.

Entry into Maritime (1995–1999)

Following my discharge, I returned to civilian life in Baltimore, where my parents were living, working as a bicycle messenger and mechanic while determining my next steps. The physical work was honest, the streets of Baltimore were educational in their own way, and the job gave me time to think. Ultimately, I applied at several agencies in Baltimore under a simple premise: ship agency is the greatest entry-level job in shipping, and I was willing to work long, flexible hours for modest pay to earn my place in the industry. I was immediately hired.

At T. Parker Host in Baltimore, I learned the fundamentals—coordinating with stevedores, customs officials, pilots, terminal operators, and ship's masters. The work demanded precision, clear communication, and the ability to manage multiple moving parts simultaneously. Every vessel call was a lesson in logistics, timing, and problem-solving.

Wilhelmsen Years: Mississippi River and Hurricane Katrina (1999–2006)

In December 1999, I joined Wilhelmsen Ships Agency (operating as Barwil Agencies) as Operations Manager in Norfolk. Following the merger of Wilhelmsen and Wallenius liner services, Wilhelmsen had to bifurcate all domestic assets and businesses away from the merged liner pool. Overnight, they needed a manager to handle Pan Ocean's tramp liner service to Norfolk and attend the RoRo vessels in liner service. It was a great opportunity—I built the office from scratch: IT systems, forms, processes, and accounting, until Barwil could reorganize their US headquarters to New Orleans.

During this time, I helped reestablish their presence in neighboring Baltimore and North Carolina ports. I handled Hampton Roads' first-ever semi-submersible operation loading floating military stock and a 700-foot floating dry dock. I also achieved Barwil's first coal nomination on the US East Coast. This quickly drew attention from both local and worldwide corporate management. I established a sub-agency agreement with Host and worked on a one-year project to develop Barwil's global coal services package, attending strategy meetings in South Africa and Singapore with the global executive team.

During this era, I was among the first agents to use a digital camera and cellular modems for increased responsiveness while onboard vessels, and I developed a new format of proforma and berth sheets with photos and maps— innovations that would be widely copied once made famous by Host's adoption of the formats.

In early 2002, I requested a transfer to Barwil's Mississippi River office in New Orleans. I had always viewed the Mississippi River as the doctorate program for ship agency—the holy grail of on-the-job education. My primary responsibility was river operations, a different world from coastal ports: more complex vessel movements, tighter margins, and unpredictable challenges that demanded constant adaptation.

Hurricane Katrina: August 2005

When Hurricane Katrina made landfall in New Orleans on August 29, 2005, the world changed. Most staff evacuated, leaving only essential employees. I volunteered to remain so colleagues with families could leave safely. I didn't fully anticipate what would unfold—none of us did. New Orleans flooded, communications failed, and normal infrastructure collapsed. For nearly two weeks, I coordinated vessel operations under conditions I'd never imagined in a U.S. port.

I managed vessel attendance during the storm itself and its immediate aftermath. As disaster response ramped up, I moved in and out of armed checkpoints and curfews, navigating encounters with police ensuring I wasn't a looter— the city was nearly evacuated except for first responders. I coordinated operations for three FEMA-chartered cruise ships that served as floating bases for emergency response teams, providing housing, food service, and staging areas when land-based facilities were underwater.

Perhaps most significantly, I managed the first deep-draft vessel to depart from the Mississippi River after the storm, once the Army had dragged the entire federal channel to sea for weeks and sunken obstacles. Dozens of barges and tugs had sunk; ships sat on levees; tugboats were in the middle of highways. Getting that vessel safely out required coordination with Coast Guard, pilots, terminal operators, and salvage teams—all operating under emergency conditions with limited communications.

Crisis leadership became part of my professional identity. I learned that I could function effectively under extreme pressure, coordinate complex operations with degraded infrastructure, and maintain focus when everything around me was in chaos.

Building the South Atlantic and Gulf Coast (2006–2016)

In May 2006, conditions in New Orleans remained extremely difficult. I accepted a position as Operations Manager in Fernandina Beach, Florida, for Amelia Maritime Services, then a joint venture owned by Host and the Quinn family of Miami. The company was ready to expand aggressively into Gulf Coast and southern Atlantic ports, and they wanted someone who knew those markets and could build from scratch.

I participated in a complete reorganization of Amelia and its absorption into Host once the Quinns sold their interests. We then purchased Atlantic Ship Agencies in Savannah, which I was responsible for integrating and growing. During this period, I oversaw complete staff reorganization and expansion of operations in Fernandina, Jacksonville, and Brunswick, and opened Savannah and Charleston offices. I progressed from boarding agent to operations manager to vice president to a seat on the board of directors, until the business was integrated by Host, where I was promoted to Vice President of Business Development.

During the transition, I developed the beginnings of Host's first ERP using Google's platform and set up domain and email services for Amelia to streamline administrative and accounting functions between offices.

Gulf Coast Expansion and M&A Integration

In 2011, Host purchased NSA Agencies. I led on-the-ground management and restructuring of their Mobile, New Orleans, and Houston offices, commuting weekly from Jacksonville for eight months. Again, I turned over staff, integrated and built processes, and hired and trained new employees handling weekly operations while coordinating with existing staff to ensure seamless transitions.

Terminal Development and ERP Integration

At Host, I was the company representative of the former Bethlehem Steel site into a multimodal logistics and terminal complex. I conducted market analysis and supply chain modeling for steel, wind energy, and bulk commodity strategies—infrastructure investment at massive scale requiring analysis defensible to investors, lenders, and regulators.

I helped Host develop a sabermetics-style system using the world's most advanced data subscriptions and services to build complex operational and financial models, market studies, and geospatial modeling for various terminal operations, including due diligence research on counterparties. I was viewed as Host's internal consultant and also handled special assignments: reestablishing inventory for 500,000 tons of alumina at their Baltimore terminal after an inventory system failure, shutting down a logistics business in New York, and closing Host's agency in Colombia.

I worked on projects including an RFP to develop floating stevedoring and barging infrastructure for Russal's bauxite mine in Guinea, redevelopment of a former fertilizer plant into a dedicated petcoke terminal for CITGO's Pascagoula refinery, and a tank storage, barge, pipeline, and truck rack terminal at the former Philadelphia refinery on the Delaware River. I was chief architect of Host's "Solutions Network" business model and pitch books that led to Metalmark Capital's $75 million investment.

Executive Leadership (2016–2025)

In October 2016, while continuing regional management responsibilities, I took on an additional role as Director of Business Development focusing terminal development projects. This was a strategic pivot, leveraging operational expertise to support clients planning major terminal infrastructure investments.

Despite expensive degrees and MBAs on the business development team, two essential elements were lacking: experience and digitization—both proven skills of mine over time. This would be my second family relocation, from Jacksonville to Norfolk.

In November 2018, following the abrupt exit of Finn Host after the sale from the Host family to Adam Anderson and investors, I was assigned responsibility as interim head of agency. Overnight, in addition to my day job, I had over 100 employees, $22 million in revenue, and $800 million in receivables. I was promoted to Vice President & Head of Agency with full executive responsibility for T. Parker Host's ship agency division—oversight of all 15 locations, P&L accountability, and a seat on the executive committee shaping corporate strategy.

The initial goal was immediate stabilization of both morale and commercial relationships at risk of shifting to Host's new agency operating under a required license. This required extensive team building and change management. In the first six months, I reduced overhead by nearly $2 million through operational efficiencies alone.

The scope was substantial: 110+ employees, 4,200+ annual port calls, operations spanning from New York to Brownsville, Texas, with full responsibility for service quality, customer relationships, financial performance, and operational excellence.

Digital Transformation

I led the company's digital transformation, implementing Oracle NetSuite as our enterprise-wide ERP and CRM system for 100+ users, replacing legacy systems cobbled together over decades. I also developed proprietary geospatial tracking systems for vessel monitoring, data reporting platforms for operational optimization, and KYC due diligence platforms.

COVID-19 Crisis Management

We maintained 100% operational continuity throughout the pandemic without losing business, customers, or employees. Keeping a 24-hour business and a team of over 100 employees on point and motivated during the worst climate any American business could find itself operating in will remain one of my proudest achievements.

Professional Development

Throughout my career, continuous learning has been central to my development. I've systematically studied maritime operations, logistics, supply chain management, and related disciplines through specialized industry literature. Over 30 years, I've built a professional library of 779 works spanning ship agency, chartering, maritime law, port operations, supply chain econometrics, and commodity markets.

I have extensive interest and knowledge in chartered vessel operations, charter parties, laytime, and typical associated claims and bill of lading issues. At Host, I was the company representative for the Society of Maritime Arbitrators in New York and led several pieces of agency litigation, fines, and claims. I have a future goal of becoming a regular maritime arbitrator under SDNY and have taken preliminary training.

Looking Forward

In May 2025, with terminal operations now fully matured and a long-term hire trained and implemented as head of agency, I sought the horizons. I parted ways on good terms with Host as part of their acquisition of Transmarine, exiting gardening leave in December 2025.

After 30 years focused primarily on maritime operations, I'm interested in applying this experience to broader logistics, supply chain, or infrastructure roles. The skills are transferable: operational leadership, crisis management, technology implementation, market development, stakeholder management, and team building.
