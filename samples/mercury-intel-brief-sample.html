Analyst Briefing — March 5, 2026

# Mercury Resources Daily Briefing | March 5, 2026

Geopolitical escalation in the Middle East has crystallized into material supply and logistics disruption, with the Strait of Hormuz emerging as the critical chokepoint driving commodity repricing across metals, energy, and agricultural sectors. Aluminum rallied to four-year highs following Bahrain's force majeure declaration; LPG flows through the Strait have contracted sharply with only two laden VLGCs transiting since late February; and oil has breached $84 Brent on supply risk materialization. Simultaneously, U.S. domestic LNG capacity is expanding—Cheniere's Corpus Christi now holds non-FTA approval for an additional 3.28 MMtpa—positioning American supply as a strategic alternative to disrupted Middle Eastern volumes. This structural bifurcation between constrained Gulf flows and rising U.S. export capacity is reshaping trade corridors and freight demand patterns in real time.

For traders and charterers, the commercial calculus has shifted decisively. Agricultural exporters face acute sensitivity to even marginal freight increases, narrowing margins on already-compressed spreads; the proposed UP-NS rail merger compounds inland logistics uncertainty for North American ag corridors. Energy traders should model dual scenarios: immediate VLGC redirection toward U.S. Gulf supply (supporting higher ton-mile demand and time-charter rates) alongside medium-term supply substitution that could normalize regional spreads. Collateral managers must reassess counterparty exposure to Hormuz-dependent refineries and LNG terminals; tenor extension risk is material. Trump administration Hormuz assurances have provided only partial psychological relief—insurance and naval guarantees do not eliminate political risk premiums embedded in current freight and bunker costs.

VLGCs could turn to USGC as LPG supply tightens

Middle East tensions have tightened LPG supply, with only two laden VLGCs passing through the Strait of Hormuz since February 28. With MEG exports potentially disrupted by up to 1.2mbd, VLGCs are being redeployed toward the U.S. Gulf Coast, where propane stocks remain elevated and exports hit 2.34mb in January, offering alternative supply for India and other Asian buyers.

AFEI propane swaps rose to $751/t on March 2 (21% increase Friday-to-Monday); m1-m2 swaps widened to $75/t. Only 2 laden VLGCs through Strait of Hormuz since Feb 28. MEG LPG exports at risk: 1.2mbd. U.S. LPG exports hit 2.34mb in January. PADD 3 propane stocks ~20mb above last year levels. MEG supplies 92% (~720kbd) of India's LPG imports. At least 3 VLGCs signalled redeployment from MEG to USGC.

Analysts Warn of Largest Oil Supply Disruption in History

S&P Global and BMI analysts warn that ongoing conflict between the U.S., Israel, and Iran could trigger the largest oil supply disruption in history if transit through the Strait of Hormuz is significantly disrupted or halted. Tanker traffic through the Strait has collapsed from ~60 vessels per day to just 5, with 20.8 million barrels per day of crude oil and products at risk, potentially exceeding supply losses from Russia's Ukraine invasion or Iraq's 1990 invasion of Kuwait.

Tanker transits at Strait of Hormuz dropped from ~60/day to 5/day (March 1). 20.8M barrels per day of crude and products shipped via Strait in first two months of 2026. Up to 15M barrels per day at risk if tankers halt. 82% of flow goes to Asian markets. 18% of global LNG supply transits Strait. Supply disruption of 7-8M bpd would exceed Ukraine and Kuwait invasion impacts. BMI low case: $75-$90/bbl; mid case: $90-$110/bbl; high case: $110-$130/bbl with risk of >$130/bbl.

Saudi's Biggest Oil Refinery Faces Another Attack

Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura refinery, the country's largest with 550,000 bpd capacity, faced an attempted drone attack with no reported damage, following a previous strike that forced production shutdown. Saudi Aramco is diverting crude supplies from its eastern region via pipeline to Yanbu on the Red Sea to avoid the Strait of Hormuz, though the Red Sea poses risks from Houthi attacks. Regional energy infrastructure disruptions have pushed crude oil prices near $83/barrel and European gas to three-year highs.

Ras Tanura refinery capacity 550,000 bpd. Crude oil prices near $83/barrel. European gas at three-year highs. Supply diversion from eastern region to Yanbu via pipeline to avoid Strait of Hormuz. Qatar LNG production stopped. Iraq forced into deep oil output cuts.

Cheniere Gets Non-FTA Permit for Additional Corpus Christi LNG Trains

The U.S. Department of Energy granted non-FTA export authorization for Cheniere's two-train LNG expansion (Trains 8 and 9) at Corpus Christi, adding 3.28 MMtpa of capacity and making the terminal the second-largest LNG export facility in the U.S. with total authorized export capacity of 4.45 Bcf/d. The project received FTA approval in July 2023 and Cheniere issued a full notice to proceed to contractor Bechtel Corp in June 2025.

Corpus Christi LNG authorized export capacity: 4.45 Bcf/d (second-largest in U.S.). Trains 8 and 9 expansion: 3.28 MMtpa (170 Bcf/year). Stage 3 Project: 10+ MMtpa capacity, 6 MMtpa operational as of end-2025. U.S. LNG exports: 15.0 Bcf/d in 2025, up from 0.5 Bcf/d in 2016. Export authorization through 2050 plus 3-year carve-out.

Aluminum price surges to 4-year high on Bahrain force majeure

Aluminum prices surged to a 4-year high following Aluminium Bahrain's (Alba) force majeure declaration due to shipping disruptions through the Strait of Hormuz amid regional conflicts. The world's largest aluminum smelter outside China has halted shipments, with over 5 million tonnes of metal normally passing through the critical waterway annually, threatening widespread supply disruptions and potential price spikes to $3,600/tonne.

Aluminum LME price +2.5% to $3,340/tonne (4-year high). Alba produced 1.62M tonnes in 2025. Over 5M tonnes annually pass through Strait of Hormuz. Goldman Sachs projects potential $3,600/tonne if regional production lost for one month. YTD aluminum up 9%.
