# Market Intelligence Briefing – March 5, 2026

Middle Eastern geopolitical tensions are fundamentally reshaping global commodity flows and port logistics. The Strait of Hormuz remains under acute pressure following regional military escalations, with Aluminium Bahrain declaring force majeure and Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex taken offline. Oil prices have surged to $84 Brent on supply disruption fears, while LPG shipments through the Gulf have collapsed—only two laden VLGCs passing since February 28. Simultaneously, Iranian drone activity against Saudi refineries and proposed Trump administration naval escorts signal sustained volatility rather than imminent resolution. This disruption is diverting VLGCs toward the U.S. Gulf Coast and driving aggressive spot rate inflation across energy logistics.

For operators and charterers, margin compression is acute across agricultural and containerized exports, where even modest freight increases risk pricing commodities out of global markets. Energy clients face both opportunity and risk: crude tanker and LNG rates will remain elevated, but U.S. Gulf LNG capacity is expanding (Cheniere's new trains add 3.28 MMtpa), potentially offering arbitrage against constrained Middle Eastern supplies. Rail consolidation pressures—particularly the UP-NS merger debate—threaten inland logistics efficiency. Expect sustained premium rates through Q2 on energy vessels; lock agricultural contract terms now before further escalation.

VLGCs could turn to USGC as LPG supply tightens

Middle East tensions have tightened LPG supply, with only two laden VLGCs passing through the Strait of Hormuz since February 28. With MEG exports potentially disrupted by up to 1.2mbd, VLGCs are being redeployed toward the U.S. Gulf Coast, where propane stocks remain elevated and exports hit 2.34mb in January, offering alternative supply for India and other Asian buyers.

Key data: AFEI propane swaps rose to $751/t on March 2 (21% increase Friday-to-Monday); m1-m2 swaps widened to $75/t. Only 2 laden VLGCs through Strait of Hormuz since Feb 28. MEG LPG exports at risk: 1.2mbd. U.S. LPG exports hit 2.34mb in January. PADD 3 propane stocks ~20mb above last year levels. MEG supplies 92% (~720kbd) of India's LPG imports. At least 3 VLGCs signalled redeployment from MEG to USGC.

Analysts Warn of Largest Oil Supply Disruption in History

S&P Global and BMI analysts warn that ongoing conflict between the U.S., Israel, and Iran could trigger the largest oil supply disruption in history if transit through the Strait of Hormuz is significantly disrupted or halted. Tanker traffic through the Strait has collapsed from ~60 vessels per day to just 5, with 20.8 million barrels per day of crude oil and products at risk, potentially exceeding supply losses from Russia's Ukraine invasion or Iraq's 1990 invasion of Kuwait.

Key data: Tanker transits at Strait of Hormuz dropped from ~60/day to 5/day (March 1). 20.8M barrels per day of crude and products shipped via Strait in first two months of 2026. Up to 15M barrels per day at risk if tankers halt. 82% of flow goes to Asian markets. 18% of global LNG supply transits Strait. Supply disruption of 7-8M bpd would exceed Ukraine and Kuwait invasion impacts. BMI low case: $75-$90/bbl; mid case: $90-$110/bbl; high case: $110-$130/bbl with risk of >$130/bbl.

Saudi's Biggest Oil Refinery Faces Another Attack

Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura refinery, the country's largest with 550,000 bpd capacity, faced an attempted drone attack with no reported damage, following a previous strike that forced production shutdown. Saudi Aramco is diverting crude supplies from its eastern region via pipeline to Yanbu on the Red Sea to avoid the Strait of Hormuz, though the Red Sea poses risks from Houthi attacks. Regional energy infrastructure disruptions have pushed crude oil prices near $83/barrel and European gas to three-year highs.

Key data: Ras Tanura refinery capacity 550,000 bpd. Crude oil prices near $83/barrel. European gas at three-year highs. Supply diversion from eastern region to Yanbu via pipeline to avoid Strait of Hormuz. Qatar LNG production stopped. Iraq forced into deep oil output cuts.

Cheniere Gets Non-FTA Permit for Additional Corpus Christi LNG Trains

The U.S. Department of Energy granted non-FTA export authorization for Cheniere's two-train LNG expansion (Trains 8 and 9) at Corpus Christi, adding 3.28 MMtpa of capacity and making the terminal the second-largest LNG export facility in the U.S. with total authorized export capacity of 4.45 Bcf/d. The project received FTA approval in July 2023 and Cheniere issued a full notice to proceed to contractor Bechtel Corp in June 2025.

Key data: Corpus Christi LNG authorized export capacity: 4.45 Bcf/d (second-largest in U.S.). Trains 8 and 9 expansion: 3.28 MMtpa (170 Bcf/year). Stage 3 Project: 10+ MMtpa capacity, 6 MMtpa operational as of end-2025. U.S. LNG exports: 15.0 Bcf/d in 2025, up from 0.5 Bcf/d in 2016. Export authorization through 2050 plus 3-year carve-out.

Aluminum price surges to 4-year high on Bahrain force majeure

Aluminum prices surged to a 4-year high following Aluminium Bahrain's (Alba) force majeure declaration due to shipping disruptions through the Strait of Hormuz amid regional conflicts. The world's largest aluminum smelter outside China has halted shipments, with over 5 million tonnes of metal normally passing through the critical waterway annually, threatening widespread supply disruptions and potential price spikes to $3,600/tonne.

Key data: Aluminum LME price +2.5% to $3,340/tonne (4-year high). Alba produced 1.62M tonnes in 2025. Over 5M tonnes annually pass through Strait of Hormuz. Goldman Sachs projects potential $3,600/tonne if regional production lost for one month. YTD aluminum up 9%.
